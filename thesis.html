Abstract

Online or streaming algorithms update statistical quantities using only the most recent observation and the current estimate. This avoids storing the entire dataset and eliminates the need to recompute sums from scratch. This thesis presents the ideas behind online formulas for the mean and the variance, together with discussions on recurrence relations, numerical stability, and convergence properties. We illustrate the behavior of these streaming estimators on simulated data and comment on their practicality in real-time environments.

Introduction

In classical statistics, most formulas assume that the entire dataset is available at once. While this approach is perfectly suited for offline analysis, it becomes inefficient when data arrive gradually or continuously. In such settings, it may be impossible—or computationally expensive—to recompute quantities each time a new value appears.

Online statistical algorithms address this issue by updating estimates incrementally. Instead of storing all past observations, they keep only a small number of running values such as the current mean or a set of recurrence coefficients. This idea dates back to early work in numerical analysis and has seen a resurgence due to the rise of streaming data sources, sensor networks, and real-time monitoring systems.
