Abstract

Online or streaming algorithms update statistical quantities using only the most recent observation and the current estimate. This avoids storing the entire dataset and eliminates the need to recompute sums from scratch. This thesis presents the ideas behind online formulas for the mean and the variance, together with discussions on recurrence relations, numerical stability, and convergence properties. We illustrate the behavior of these streaming estimators on simulated data and comment on their practicality in real-time environments.

Introduction

In classical statistics, most formulas assume that the entire dataset is available at once. While this approach is perfectly suited for offline analysis, it becomes inefficient when data arrive gradually or continuously. In such settings, it may be impossible—or computationally expensive—to recompute quantities each time a new value appears.

Online statistical algorithms address this issue by updating estimates incrementally. Instead of storing all past observations, they keep only a small number of running values such as the current mean or a set of recurrence coefficients. This idea dates back to early work in numerical analysis and has seen a resurgence due to the rise of streaming data sources, sensor networks, and real-time monitoring systems.

Statistical Background

Let a stream of data be denoted by:

x₁, x₂, x₃, … , xₙ

Our goal is to compute classical quantities such as:

  • Mean: μₙ = (1/n) Σ xᵢ
  • Variance: σₙ² = (1/n) Σ (xᵢ − μₙ)²

Computing these directly requires recomputing sums every time a new value arrives. The purpose of online formulas is to avoid this recomputation.

Streaming Mean

The recurrence for the streaming mean is built on the observation that when a new value xₙ arrives, the new mean μₙ can be obtained from the previous one μₙ₋₁:

μₙ = μₙ₋₁ + (xₙ − μₙ₋₁) / n

This elegant formula shows how the mean shifts gradually toward the new value. It is computationally efficient and numerically stable.

Streaming mean update: Old mean: μₙ₋₁ New value: xₙ Adjustment: (xₙ − μₙ₋₁) / n Updated mean: μₙ

Streaming Variance (Welford’s Algorithm)

The standard definition of variance is not suitable for streaming because it depends on the mean, which itself is changing. Welford’s method resolves this by tracking an auxiliary quantity:

M₂ₙ = Σ (xᵢ − μₙ)²

The recurrence is:

δ = xₙ − μₙ₋₁ μₙ = μₙ₋₁ + δ / n M₂ₙ = M₂ₙ₋₁ + δ (xₙ − μₙ)

Variance is then:

σₙ² = M₂ₙ / n

This method is widely regarded as one of the most stable ways to compute variance online.

Numerical Stability

Online estimators must be resistant to rounding errors, especially when the data stream grows large. The classical variance formula:

σ² = (Σ xᵢ² / n) − μ²

is prone to catastrophic cancellation. Welford’s recurrence avoids this because the updates rely on incremental differences rather than subtracting large numbers.

Problematic: large numbers − large numbers → small difference Stable: update via small corrections each step

Simulation Study

To understand the behavior of streaming estimators, we simulate a sequence of 5,000 values from a normal distribution:

xᵢ ~ N(0, 1)

At each step we record the streaming mean and compare it with the true mean. The convergence pattern looks like:

μₙ | 0.2| ┌─ fluctuations ─┐ 0.1| ┌─────┘ └──────┐ 0.0|───────┘ Stable limit −0.1| −0.2| +----------------------------------------------→ n 0 1000 2000 3000 4000

The variance estimator shows a similar stabilization once n becomes sufficiently large.

Applications

Sensor Streams

Temperature or vibration sensors produce rapid streams of measurements. Online mean and variance track anomalies without storing large logs.

Network Monitoring

Packet arrival times, round-trip delays, or throughput measures are well suited to streaming statistics.

Cybersecurity

Anomaly detection systems often rely on streaming statistical estimators to identify unusual behavior in real time.

Page 8

Conclusion

Online algorithms provide an elegant and efficient way to compute statistics in real time. The mean and variance can be updated using simple recurrence relations that avoid storing past data and reduce numerical errors. As data streams continue to grow in importance, these methods remain fundamental tools for modern statistical analysis and real-time decision systems.
