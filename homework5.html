1. Introduction

This homework explores the Measures of Location and Dispersion, and introduces online algorithms for calculating mean and variance efficiently. These concepts are essential in understanding how data is summarized, compared, and updated dynamically when new information becomes available.

2. Measures of Location

Measures of location (also called measures of central tendency) describe the center or typical value of a dataset. The main ones include:

  • Arithmetic Mean: The sum of all observations divided by their number. Sensitive to outliers but widely used in symmetric distributions.
  • Median: The middle value when the data is ordered. Robust to outliers and skewed data.
  • Mode: The most frequent observation. Useful for categorical data.
  • Geometric Mean: The nth root of the product of n values. Suitable for growth rates, percentages, or multiplicative processes.
  • Harmonic Mean: The reciprocal of the average of reciprocals. Ideal for averaging rates or ratios (e.g., speed).

3. Measures of Dispersion

Dispersion measures describe the spread or variability of the data. The most common ones are:

  • Range: The difference between the maximum and minimum value.
  • Variance (σ²): The average of squared deviations from the mean.
  • Standard Deviation (σ): The square root of variance, measured in the same units as the data.
  • Interquartile Range (IQR): The difference between the 75th and 25th percentiles, describing the middle 50% of data.
  • Median Absolute Deviation (MAD): The median of absolute deviations from the median. Robust against outliers.
  • Coefficient of Variation (CV): The ratio of SD to the mean, useful for comparing variability between datasets of different scales.

Common Pitfalls

  • Outliers can drag the mean; prefer median or a trimmed mean for heavy-tailed data.
  • Zeros/negatives invalidate geometric and harmonic means.
  • Range is fragile; report it with IQR or SD.
  • Always pair a spread measure with a center (e.g., mean±SD or median+IQR).

Numerical Stability: Batch vs Online

Online formulas, such as Welford’s update, process one observation at a time, requiring constant memory and avoiding catastrophic cancellation. Traditional batch computation (recomputing all sums each time) is slower, more memory-intensive, and prone to round-off errors on large datasets. Online algorithms are thus preferred for real-time data or large-scale applications.
